Every four years, the volunteers who write the entry-level amateur radio exam retire the old question pool and release a new one. The one that [took effect July 1](https://www.arrl.org/news/new-technician-question-pool-takes-effect-july-1) is the first refresh since 2022, and it leans toward a specific kind of question: digital tools built for exactly the moment cell service disappears.

The Technician class is the standard first step into ham radio: no Morse code, one 35-question multiple-choice exam, and privileges that already cover VHF/UHF repeaters plus a slice of HF. It's a different license from GMRS, the $35, no-exam option built for a household or small group that just wants handhelds with more range than a store-bought walkie-talkie — Technician asks more of an operator and grants more in return.

The [National Conference of Volunteer Examiner Coordinators' Question Pool Committee](https://ncvec.org/index.php/2026-2030-technician-question-pool) released the new pool on December 18, 2025, for use in all exam sessions starting July 1, 2026. It trims the question count slightly, from 412 to 409, with 27 new questions, 30 retired, and roughly 155 reworded for clarity.

The additions are the interesting part. [Winlink](https://hambook.org/tech2022/appendx/supplement-whats-new-in-the-2026-question-pool/), the system that sends and receives email over HF radio with no internet in the path, now appears explicitly as an emergency-communications tool rather than a footnote — the kind of thing a net uses to relay a resource request once the cell towers and the power lines feeding them are both down. DMR, a digital voice mode, is now tested on the mechanics an operator actually needs day to day: code plugs, the configuration file loaded onto a digital radio, and color codes, the access key that keeps one repeater's traffic from colliding with a neighboring repeater sharing the same channel. FT8, a weak-signal digital mode that can pull a readable exchange out of noise no voice signal would survive, is tested generically now instead of tied to one piece of software — the mode has outlasted any single app built for it, and the question wording finally reflects that.

None of this changes what a Technician license permits; the privileges are the same as they were in June. What changed is what a newcomer is expected to understand before getting on the air, and it now points at the tools an operator reaches for once the ordinary infrastructure has already failed. A question pool is a fair record of what its writers think is worth knowing, and this one puts emergency digital modes near the top of the list.
