PG&E cut power to about 7,800 customers across 10 counties on July 15 — the utility's [third Public Safety Power Shutoff of the year](https://investor.pgecorp.com/news-events/press-releases/press-release-details/2026/PGE-Monitoring-High-Wind-Event-Is-Preparing-for-Possible-Public-Safety-Power-Shutoff-for-Portions-of-10-Counties/default.aspx). Calaveras and Tuolumne weren't on that list, but both counties sit in the same [Tier 2 and Tier 3 high fire-threat districts](https://www.cpuc.ca.gov/industries-and-topics/wildfires/fire-threat-maps-and-fire-safety-rulemaking) PG&E uses to decide who goes next when wind, low humidity, and dry fuel line up. That puts a shutoff reaching the Highway 4 or Highway 49 corridor well within reach this season — worth planning around before an outage starts, not after.

## The backup clock

A [2020 California Public Utilities Commission decision](https://www.cpuc.ca.gov/industries-and-topics/internet-and-phone/network-performance-and-public-safety/communications-network-resiliency/communications-resiliency-plans) requires wireless carriers to keep cell sites in Tier 2 and Tier 3 high fire-threat districts running on backup power for at least 72 hours after the grid goes down — enough to hold 911 access, emergency alerts, and basic connectivity through a typical shutoff. That is a real, enforceable floor.

It is also just a floor. PG&E's own [July 15 shutoff notice](https://investor.pgecorp.com/news-events/press-releases/press-release-details/2026/PGE-Monitoring-High-Wind-Event-Is-Preparing-for-Possible-Public-Safety-Power-Shutoff-for-Portions-of-10-Counties/default.aspx) is candid that outage length depends on real-time weather, damage assessments, and the patrols and repairs needed before lines are safe to re-energize — and in steep terrain, that inspection can take longer than flat ground. When a shutoff runs past 72 hours, the backup clock at the cell site runs out before the grid comes back, whether or not anything else goes wrong.

## What closes the gap

None of this needs to be solved in the moment. A few things are worth having ready before a Red Flag forecast, not after the outage starts:

- **A battery or hand-crank radio that receives NOAA Weather Radio or standard AM/FM.** It pulls official alerts straight off the air, with no cell tower, account, or app in the chain.
- **A charged battery bank or a small solar panel**, topped off ahead of a forecast wind event rather than found dead when the power drops.
- **A GMRS radio.** The FCC license is a [flat $35 for ten years, with no exam](https://www.fcc.gov/personal-service-and-amateur-application-fees), covers an entire household under one callsign, and talks radio-to-radio between family, neighbors, and vehicles without touching the phone network at all.

PG&E posts a rolling [7-day PSPS outlook](https://www.pge.com/pspsweather) and address-level shutoff status once a watch is called — that page is the one to check as conditions build, not a blog post written days or weeks ahead of the next event. The backup-power window is generous. It is still a window, and the plan for what happens after it closes is the part worth having ready now.
