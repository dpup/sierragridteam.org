A canyon with one road out and thin cell coverage is a familiar setup in the Calaveras and Tuolumne foothills. It is also the exact problem two mountain communities elsewhere have been solving with volunteer radio, and their networks are worth a look at the height of fire season — because the part that makes them work is not the equipment.

## The fast part, and the slow part

In May, residents of Weber Canyon in Utah's Summit County [switched on a solar-powered GMRS repeater](https://www.kpcw.org/summit-county/2026-07-13/summit-county-residents-build-two-way-radio-network-for-fire-season) built on a donated half-century-old television tower on the canyon's northern ridgeline. GMRS — the General Mobile Radio Service, a licensed UHF two-way band — carries voice between handhelds with no cell tower in the path, and a single ridgeline repeater extends that range across a whole watershed; the Weber Canyon net now reaches out to Oakley, Kamas, and Park City. The project began after a resident watched a documentary about the 2018 Camp Fire and recognized his own canyon in it: sparse cell service, one way in and out. The [license is a flat $35 for ten years](https://www.fcc.gov/personal-service-and-amateur-application-fees), no exam, and the repeater itself needed FCC approval.

The radio was the fast part. About 30 people now check in on a monthly roll call — first Tuesday, 8 p.m. That standing net is the point. A radio that has sat in a drawer since the day it was bought is not a communications plan; a group that keys up together every month, on a known channel at a known time, is. The roll call is where a member learns whether their handheld actually reaches the repeater from their own property — which is not something to find out during an evacuation.

## The longer proof

California's own fire country has the longer-running version. After the 2015 Valley Fire destroyed 1,300 structures and killed 4 people in Lake County, survivors in Cobb built the Cobb Alert Net, [documented by the Fire Adapted Communities Learning Network](https://fireadaptednetwork.org/a-community-volunteer-radio-network-that-can/). It now counts 270 participants across 85 square miles and 7 hamlets, organized in three tiers: a small core of licensed amateur ("ham") radio operators, 34 GMRS operators who serve as neighborhood leaders, and residents who use the simplest license-free Family Radio Service walkie-talkies. It practices weekly, 20 to 60 minutes at a time.

The tiers are what let it scale. A neighbor can join at whatever level they are ready for — an FRS radio off a store shelf, a $35 GMRS license, or a full ham ticket — without being left out of the net. Nobody has to clear the highest bar to be reachable.

## What carries across

Two things translate to the Highway 4 and Highway 49 corridors. First, the hardware is the cheap, quick part; the network is the people who practice with it. Second, both nets were built deliberately, before the season they were meant for — the same order of operations as clearing defensible space or packing a go-bag. A GMRS license covers an entire household and takes no exam, which makes it the low doorway in. The higher-value step is finding out whether your road, ridge, or water district already runs a net — and if it doesn't, knowing that a handful of neighbors and one repeater is all Cobb and Weber Canyon started with.

Both communities stood up their nets in an ordinary month rather than a dangerous one. The monthly or weekly schedule is what turned a pile of radios into a network that answers.
